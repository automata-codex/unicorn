# Mental Health Toolkit

This isn't a pep talk. It's a reference document for when your brain starts doing the thing. Find your trigger, read what you need, then get back to work—or don't, if rest is what's actually called for.

---

## The Frame: Goals vs. Hopes

Before you read anything else, get this distinction locked in:

**Goals** are things you can control. Finish the GM context architecture. Run a playtest. Fix the oracle data handling. Write the rules lookup tool.

**Hopes** are other people's behavior. Players love it. It sells well. Someone leaves a glowing review. The community adopts it.

You can work toward hopes. You can't guarantee them. When you treat a hope like a goal—when "this feels exactly like a real GM" becomes your success criterion—you've handed your sense of accomplishment to an impossible standard. That's a recipe for anxiety, because you've defined success as something you can't actually make happen.

When you're stressed, ask: **Is this a goal or a hope?** If it's a hope, find the goal underneath it. That's the thing you can actually do.

## When Perfectionism Is Running the Show

*You know this one. The "it has to feel completely human" loop. The sense that every rough edge is proof the whole thing is wrong.*

### Quick Anchors

- The bar is "better than not playing." That's always been the bar.
- It will always feel like an AI, to one degree or another. That's not failure. That's an accurate description of the product.
- You're in early iteration mode. This is craft-shaping, not acceptance-seeking.

### When You're Deep In It

Here's what's actually happening: you learned early that worth comes from recognition, from being the gifted kid who gets it right. That wiring doesn't just disappear. But it's running an old program on new hardware, and it's not serving you here.

The perfectionism isn't protecting your work. It's protecting you from the possibility of rejection—by making sure nothing ever gets finished enough to be rejected. That's not quality control. That's avoidance wearing a craftsman's apron.

Zoltar doesn't need to be unimpeachable. It needs to exist and be useful. The counterfactual isn't a perfect human GM—it's the scenario never getting played at all. A real, imperfect thing that lets someone run a Mothership session on a Tuesday night teaches you more than a perfect thing in your head ever could.

Release it at "good enough." Learn what you learn. Make the next iteration better. That's the only way this works.

### Grounding Questions

- What specific flaw am I afraid someone will find? (Name it. It's usually less damning out loud.)
- If a friend showed me work at this level, would I tell them it wasn't ready?
- Am I polishing, or am I hiding?
- Is the outcome I'm chasing a goal or a hope? (Hint: "everyone loves it" is a hope. "I finish it" is a goal.)

## When Iteration Anxiety Takes Over

*The "how many playtests until it's right?" spiral. The dread of not knowing the distance between here and done.*

### Quick Anchors

- "How many iterations?" is not a useful question. The number is unknowable and the bar keeps moving.
- The useful question is: did the last run surface feedback I can act on? If yes, the process is working.
- "Working" is the only success criterion for an iteration.

### When You're Deep In It

The iteration question feels like it's about engineering rigor, but it's usually anxiety in disguise. What you're really asking is: *will this ever be good enough?* And that question doesn't have a technical answer, because "good enough" isn't a fixed target—it's a feeling your perfectionism controls.

Here's the reframe: each playtest has a specific job. Playtest one asked "does the core loop hold together?" It answered yes. Playtest two is asking something more specific—did the oracle changes work, does the revised context window architecture feel better? Those are answerable questions. Answer them, incorporate the results, define the next question. That's the whole process.

You don't need to see all the way to done. You just need to see the next iteration clearly.

### Grounding Questions

- What specific hypotheses am I testing in this playtest? (If I can't name them, I'm not ready to run it yet.)
- Did the last playtest surface actionable feedback? If yes, is the process working?
- Am I evaluating the iteration, or am I just feeling afraid and calling it analysis?

## When Market Anxiety Takes Over

*Fear of disappearing into a crowded market. Obsessing over whether anyone will care. "There are already AI GMs, why would anyone use mine?"*

### Quick Anchors

- You can't control reception. You can only control whether the work exists.
- Zoltar is genuinely novel. No one has built this architecture.
- The open core model means the self-hosted version exists regardless of SaaS success.

### When You're Deep In It

Let's be honest about what market anxiety actually is: it's trying to pre-feel rejection so it won't surprise you. Your brain thinks if it can just accurately predict failure, it'll hurt less. It won't. And the prediction is probably wrong anyway—you don't have enough information to know how this will land.

The TTRPG tools market is active, yes. But "active" means proven demand. People are buying things, using things, looking for things. Your job isn't to out-shout everyone else; it's to make something real and put it where people can find it. That's the goal. The rest is hope.

Zoltar doesn't need to be the most exciting thing in the market. It needs to be *yours*—with your architecture, your voice, your weird specific interest in solo play and derelict spaceships. That's the differentiation. Not a gimmick. Not a hook. Just the fact that you built it and someone else didn't.

And if it doesn't land the way you hoped? You'll have learned how to ship. You'll have a finished product. You'll know more than you know now. That's not failure. That's the cost of entry.

### Grounding Questions

- Am I actually evaluating the market, or am I just feeling afraid and calling it analysis?
- What would I do if I knew this would land moderately well—not a hit, not a flop? Would I still make it?
- What's the worst realistic outcome, and can I live with it?
- Is what I'm stressed about a goal or a hope? What's the goal underneath the hope?

## When Deadline Pressure Is Burning You Out

*You know this is a short trip to burnout town. And yet.*

### Quick Anchors

- Steady progress on something meaningful. Not aggressive timelines.
- You can always ease down. Nothing breaks.
- A deadline you set yourself is a guess, not a promise.

### When You're Deep In It

You've already identified this pattern clearly: deadline pressure leads to burnout. This isn't a mystery. This isn't something you need to troubleshoot. You just need to actually believe yourself when you say it.

The urgency you're feeling right now—where is it coming from? Is there an actual external constraint, or did you invent a timeline and then start treating it like a blood oath? Because those are different situations requiring different responses.

If it's external: okay, triage. What actually has to happen, and what can slip?

If it's internal: you made it up. You can unmake it. "I'll have this ready by X" is not a commitment to anyone. It's a guess you made when you felt ambitious. Guesses can be revised.

Zoltar will get done faster if you don't burn out halfway through. Sustainable pace isn't laziness. It's strategy. Your brain works better when it's not running on fumes and cortisol.

### Grounding Questions

- Who am I actually accountable to for this timeline? (If the answer is "me," that's not accountability. That's pressure cosplaying as discipline.)
- What would "steady progress" look like today, if I let go of the deadline?
- Am I tired, or am I pushing through tired? What do I actually need right now?

## When Context Switching Is Draining You

*Discord. Community engagement. The tax of task-switching with ADHD.*

### Quick Anchors

- Strategic engagement, not constant engagement.
- Hyperfocus is a feature. Protect it.
- The architecture work requires deep focus. Guard it accordingly.

### When You're Deep In It

Here's the thing about context switching with ADHD: it's not just annoying, it's expensive. Every switch costs cognitive resources. And the kind of engagement that social platforms reward—quick responses, constant presence, always being "on"—is designed for brains that don't pay that tax. Yours does.

You don't have to play that game. Building an audience doesn't require being perpetually available. It requires showing up with something valuable, periodically, in places where people can find it. That's it.

Your hyperfocus is an asset when you're deep in the context window architecture or tuning the oracle system. It's a liability when platforms are trying to fragment your attention into tiny dopamine-chasing pieces. Protect the asset. Let the community stuff be strategic and bounded, not constant and draining.

### Grounding Questions

- What's my actual goal with community engagement right now? (Be specific.)
- Is this task moving me toward that goal, or is it just something that feels productive?
- What would I do with this time if I gave myself permission to skip the engagement stuff today?

## When Creative Work Starts Feeling Like Work-Work

*The resistance that shows up once stakes enter the picture. The thing you loved becoming the thing you dread.*

### Quick Anchors

- You're building a tool you personally want to exist. Lower the stakes.
- The goal is to live as a maker, not to produce finished products on demand.
- You started this because you wanted to play more TTRPGs. Don't lose that.

### When You're Deep In It

This is the real tension, and it's not going to fully resolve. Creative work and commercial pressure are in friction. The thing that makes creative work nourishing—intrinsic motivation, play, following curiosity—is exactly what market demands tend to erode. That's not a personal failing. That's the structure you're operating in.

But here's what you can control: the frame you put around the work.

"I'm launching a product" activates all the achievement/rejection wiring. "I'm building a thing I genuinely want to use, and other people might want it too" doesn't. Same work, different psychological container. Use the container that lets you actually do the work.

:::alex-comment
Try framing it as "I'm sharing something I love and am proud of with the world," not "I'm launching a product."
:::

You're not running a content factory. You're a person who makes things, and sometimes those things go out into the world, and sometimes people pay for them. The making is the point. The rest is logistics.

If Zoltar starts feeling like a job you hate, that's a signal to check your frame. Have you accidentally turned "thing I'm excited about" into "thing I must produce to prove my worth"? If so, reframe. You're allowed to do that as many times as you need.

### Grounding Questions

- What excited me about this project before stakes got attached?
- Am I working on this because I want to, or because I feel like I have to?
- If no one ever bought this, would I still be glad I made it?

## Emergency Reset

*For when you're too deep in the spiral to even identify which trigger it is.*

### Do This First

1. Step away from the screen. Seriously. Go get water, stand outside for two minutes, something.
2. Name what you're feeling in your body. Not the thoughts—the physical sensation. Chest tight? Jaw clenched? Shoulders up around your ears?
3. Ask yourself: Am I actually in danger right now, or does my nervous system just think I am?

### Then Read This

You are not your productivity. Your worth is not contingent on whether this project succeeds, ships, sells, or gets praised. You are a person who makes things because making things is part of who you are. That's already true. It doesn't require proof.

Zoltar is a project. It's not a referendum on your value as a human being. It's not even a referendum on your value as a creator. It's just a thing you're making. It will be done when it's done. It will be what it is. And you will still be you afterward, regardless of how it goes.

Right now, in this moment, nothing is on fire. You have time. You have options. You can ease down, adjust, take a break, change direction, or keep going—all of those are available to you. The only thing that's not available is perfection, and that was never on the table anyway.

Breathe. Then decide what you actually want to do next. Not what you "should" do. What you want.
